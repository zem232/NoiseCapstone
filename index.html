In recent years, the growing volume of noise complaints in New York City has increased response times and hindered enforcement of the city’s noise code by the Department of Environmental Protection. In this Capstone project, we used supervised learning to assign complaint priority and produced an interactive heatmap to optimize response to noise complaints.

Noisy New York City

The noise code was created to reduce excessive and unreasonable noises. The city's enforcement of the noise code is primarily complaint-based. Residents register a complaint via the 311 hotline and the complaint is directed to the relevant agency.

  • NYC Noise Code

NYC DEP

New York City Department of Environmental Protection (NYC DEP) handles complaints relating to equipment or repeating noise.

The Problem

Noise complaints have increased by 20% since 2014. Enforcement of the noise code has been hampered due to inability to handle sheer volumes.

In 2018, the DEP could not verify noise was occuring in 55% of noise complaints.

Next

The Goal

The primary motivation of this project is to provide a data-driven approach to:
  1. Increase efficiency of complaints successfully closed
  2. Decrease complaint response times

DOB Construction & After-Hour Variance Permits

Existing literature found that neighborhood noise complaints are significantly associated with major constructions.
Department of Building’s (DOB) construction permit and After Hour Variance (AHV) were selected as a proxy to construction location and construction noise. DOB permits was found to be a bad proxy for construction noise and noise code enforceability. Sponsors highlighted that the presence of AHV in the vicinity of a noise complaint tend to increase enforceability based on field experience.

Census Demographic Data

High frequency of 311 noise complaints have been found to be positively correlated to Lower Manhattan’s high income and education demographic feature. A geographically weighted regression analysis did not find any relationship between demographic features and ACS Survey and complaint time.

Land Use Data

Land use has been found to affect noise tolerance and noise complaints. Primary Land Use Tax Lot Output (PLUTO) was utilized to obtain New York City’s land use and geographic data at tax lot level. Different types of noise complaints have been found to correspond to various land use types, such as higher percentage of dog barking complaints in residential and public spaces.

Prediction

LSTM Neural Network

A Long short-term memory (LSTM) Neural Network model was developed with 49 spatiotemporal features including weather and after-hour variance permits, as well as 28 autoregressive features.

Overall, the NN provided a good estimate of the true standardized mean but failed to capture spikes in complaint variance. Our findings suggest that a NN model can learn the baseline volume (including periodicity) of complaints within targeted spatial regions but struggles with predicting specific complaint clusters.

Optimization

Using unsupervised learning to cluster complaints

To optimize the inspector's response to current backlog of open complaints, we originally used the ST-DBSCAN method to cluster complaints within fixed temporal and spatial constraints.
With discussion with the spinsors, we discovered that an interactive heat map for visualiation was preferred over this clustering method.

Next

Prioritization

Classifying Priority with Random Forest

We trained a random forest classifier on 311 complaint outcomes from 2016 and tested on July 2019 complaints. A probability score was assigned to each complaint based on its likelihood of enforcement. We optimized for model recall based on sponsor preference.
In evaluation, the model precision was 9.3% and recall was 91.1%.

Sponsor Feedback

To ensure our project aligned with DEP’s priorities, we met every 1-2 months with our sponsors, consisting of DEP inspectors and managers. In summer 2019, we presented our neural network and our binary classification models. DEP chose the classification model to use due to its ability to be leveraged in real time, its potential to reduce response time, and its greater usefulness to everyday inspector operations.
Sponsor feedback shaped every aspect of the project. For example, DEP requested that information about after-hours construction be added to the model, so we wrote a web scraper to get the data and feed it to the random forest classifier. DEP was previously using a basic heatmap to show noise complaints; we built a full interactive visualization to surface complaints, including their modeled probabilities. Inspectors picked out the dashboard filters and the metrics that are useful to them.

Conclusion

Our main goal was to develop a data-driven approach to optimize the DEP's scheduling of noise complaint inspections. The two methods which we explored were to optimize the response to the current backlog of open complaints by prioritization, as well as optimizing future scheduling with prediction. Based off of our research and feedback from the sponsors, we determined that optimizing the response to current complaints by visualizing priorities of current complaint backlog on a heatmap is the most useful and practical approach.
